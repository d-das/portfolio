Reflections on Civilizations



  Fernand Braudel, the famous French Historian and promminant memeber of "École des Annales" spent 5 years as a prisoner of war in Lübeck, Germany, up until the end of the Second World War. While in captivity he wrote most of his landmark thesis La Méditerranée et le Monde Méditerranéen à l'Epoque de Philippe II (1949) (The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II) — partly, he said, as a 'direct existential response to the tragic times I was passing through.'
He remarks:

"All those occurrences which poured in upon us from the radio and the newspapers of our enemies, or even the news from London which our clandestine recievers gave us — I had to outdistance, reject, deny them. Down with occurrences, especially the vexing ones! I had to believe that history, destiny, was written at a much more profound level. Choosing a long time scale to observe from was choosing the position of God the Father himself as refuge. Far removed from our persons and our daily misery, history was being made, shifting slowly, as slowly as the ancient life of the Mediterranean, whose perdurability and majestic immobility had so often moved me."

  Thus Braudel approach history as something almost transcendental — seeking a deeper, more profound understanding of what is happening beneath the confusing flury of the occurrences we witness in our lifetime. He describes these singular events in history, no matter how striking, absurd, or important, as "crests of foam that the tides of history cary on their stormy backs." Immediatley called to attention then, is the personal and dialectical relationships that the individual has with history. On this note, Braudel writes:

"Man do not make history, rather it is history above all that makes man and thereby absolves them from blame."
